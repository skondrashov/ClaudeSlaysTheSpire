## Earned vs luck — the verdict

**EARNED.** The case, with the survivorship-bias guard applied:

1. **The engine was a five-decision construction against a self-diagnosed gap**, not a windfall. F15 names the missing Str engine; F24/F27/F30/F34/F40/F46 each buy/smith/draft one named component with the mechanism stated before the payoff existed. The shop/reward OFFER stream is the luck component (Brimstone in the F24 shop, Heavy Blade at F30, Snecko offered F34) — offer luck of roughly normal-good magnitude, and conversion was 6-for-6.
2. **No key fight hinged on a low-probability draw.** Collector: burst staged via potion + Offering (deterministic digs). AO: P1 lethal and the 222 finisher both computed on cards already located via Offering; Impervious+ for Soul Strike was planned at F49. The deck carried FOUR independent finisher lines (HB+, Whirlwind+, Immolate, BfB) and used each as primary lethal in at least one fight — redundancy is the opposite of variance-dependence.
3. **Snecko rolls cut both ways and roughly canceled**: the F36 "taxed everything to 3E" turn cost ~14-20 HP; the F42/F50 0E jackpots saved comparable amounts. Draw-7 over a 24-card deck of mostly-cheap cards makes good turns the design median, not luck.
4. **Honest luck inventory:** Awakened One as the Act 3 boss is the deck's best matchup (known from F34 and consciously exploited, including the F37 Metallicize skip); the burning elite rolled onto the easier-for-this-deck Nob; no Time Eater / no Champ. Boss-identity luck is real but was PLAYED, not merely received.
5. **Anti-luck for the record:** burning-elite Nob (harder variant), Bird-Faced Urn dud from Mind Bloom, the Snecko F36 tax, Emerald Key and Red Mask both eaten by interface failures.

Score: deck quality earned the short fights; short fights are what denies variance its turns. Not a high-variance win.
